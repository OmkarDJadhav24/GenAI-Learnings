Part 2: POS Tagging in NLP: How to Label Parts of Speech Using Python

Want to make your NLP pipeline smarter? Start by teaching your machine the grammar of language! In this beginner-friendly blog, we’ll explore Part-of-Speech (POS) tagging, a crucial step that labels each word in a sentence with its role — noun, verb, adjective, and more. With hands-on examples using NLTK, you’ll learn how to build grammatically aware models in no time.

In Natural Language Processing (NLP), understanding the role of each word in a sentence is crucial. This is where Part-of-Speech (POS) tagging comes into play.

Whether you’re building a chatbot, search engine, or sentiment analyzer, POS tagging helps your model understand grammar, meaning, and context better. Let’s explore how it works and implement it using Python.

POS tagging is the process of labeling each word in a sentence with it’s correct grammatical role — such as noun, verb, adjective, etc.

For example, in the sentence:

“NLP makes machines understand language.”

The POS tags might be:

  • NLP → Noun
  • makes → Verb
  • machines → Noun
  • understand → Verb
  • language → Noun

Why is POS Tagging Important?

  • Improves lemmatization: Knowing the POS helps choose the correct lemma (base word).
  • Useful for named entity recognition (NER).
  • Enhances syntactic and semantic parsing.
  • Helps filter specific words (e.g., extracting only adjectives or verbs).

Real-World Use Cases

  • Lemmatization with correct POS: Instead of guessing, we can use POS to lemmatize accurately.
  • Chunking and Named Entity Recognition: POS tagging helps in identifying meaningful phrases like “New York City” or “Steve Jobs”.

Final Thoughts

POS tagging is a foundational technique in NLP that improves both preprocessing and downstream tasks. Whether you’re cleaning text or designing models, this step adds structure to the chaos of language.

After learning how to tag parts of speech in a sentence, the next step in understanding natural language is grouping words into meaningful phrases. This process is called chunking — and its opposite, chinking, helps refine those groups.
